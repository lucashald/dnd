Icelace Lake

Narfell's Frozen Frontier

Quick Reference

Type: Glacial Lake Region: Narfell, The Cold Lands Length: ~150 miles Width: 5-25 miles (varies) Temperature: Frigid year-round Primary Resources: Salmon, Trout, Furs

⚠️ Environmental Hazard ⚠️

Icelace Lake is deadly to the unprepared.

The waters are bitterly cold and can cause hypothermia in minutes. The ice in winter is thick but treacherous, with hidden cracks and thin spots. Predators—both natural and unnatural—are drawn to the lake's abundant wildlife.

Geography

Physical Description

Icelace Lake is a long, narrow body of water that stretches across much of northern Narfell, acting as a natural barrier between the populated regions and the Great Glacier to the north. The lake is fed entirely by glacial runoff, giving its waters their distinctive pale blue-white color and numbing cold.

Dimensions

  • Length: Approximately 150 miles from north to south
  • Width: Varies from 5 to 25 miles, widest in the central basin
  • Depth: 200-400 feet in deepest sections
  • Surface Area: Roughly 1,500 square miles

Characteristics

  • Color: Pale blue-white due to suspended glacial sediment and depth
  • Temperature: Never above 45°F even in summer; drops to freezing in winter
  • Ice Coverage: Frozen solid from late autumn through early spring (4-5 months)
  • Ice Thickness: Can reach 3-4 feet in harsh winters, 1-2 feet normally
  • Currents: Relatively calm, though underwater currents from glacial inflow can be dangerous
  • Glacial Discharge: The northern end feeds directly from three major glacial valleys

Surrounding Landscape

  • Northern Shore: Rocky, glacier-carved coastline with steep cliffs and fjords
  • Eastern Shore: Pine forests giving way to tundra as you move north
  • Western Shore: More gentle, with meadows and river valleys
  • Southern End: Transitions to rolling hills and more temperate terrain
  • Islands: Several rocky islands dot the lake, some with ruins of ancient settlements

Ecology & Wildlife

Fish Populations

Despite the harsh conditions, Icelace Lake teems with fish—the primary reason people brave its frigid waters.

Native Species

  • Arctic Salmon: Large, strong fish weighing 10-20 pounds; best in spring and fall runs
  • Lake Trout: Year-round availability; smaller than salmon but abundant
  • Char: Deep-water species; harder to catch but prized for their flavor
  • Whitefish: Small, silvery fish; used for bait and food
  • Pike: Predatory fish; aggressive and dangerous if provoked in shallow water

Fishing Seasons

  • Spring (Thaw): Salmon run; most abundant and easiest to catch
  • Summer: Good fishing in deeper waters; some danger from overheating
  • Autumn (Pre-Freeze): Final salmon run; fish are aggressive and fat
  • Winter (Frozen): Ice fishing through holes; dangerous but productive

Other Wildlife

  • Bears: Drawn to the lake by fish abundance; extremely dangerous during salmon runs
  • Wolves: Hunt along the shores, particularly in winter
  • Water Birds: Geese, ducks, loons; migrate seasonally
  • Seals: Rare, but occasionally seen in deeper northern waters
  • Otters: Scarce; hunted for their valuable pelts

Predatory Hazards

  • Giant Pike: Unnatural versions inhabit the deepest parts of the lake; 8-12 feet long
  • Lake Monsters: Rumors of something large moving in the depths, particularly in winter
  • Dire Bears: The glacial valleys occasionally produce bears of unnatural size and ferocity

Settlements & Locations

Inhabited Areas

Nar Tribes

Various tribes of Nar, the indigenous people of Narfell, have established settlements along Icelace Lake's shores. These are semi-nomadic communities that follow the seasonal fish runs.

  • Whitefin Tribe: Controls the southern and eastern shores; known for expert fishermen
  • Glacierbane Tribe: Northern settlements; hardiest and most aggressive
  • Rivermouth Tribe: Western shore settlements; traders and craftspeople
  • Seasonal Camps: Temporary fishing villages established during spawning runs

Notable Locations

  • Whitefin Landing: Largest semi-permanent settlement (150-200 inhabitants); trading hub
  • Ghost Rock Island: Ancient ruins suggest pre-Nar habitation; now considered cursed
  • The Narrows: Dangerous choke point where the lake is only 3 miles wide; strong currents
  • Salmon Falls: Where three rivers feed into the southern end; spawning ground of massive salmon
  • The Glacier Gate: Northern inlet where glacial valleys meet the lake; treacherous and beautiful

Ancient Ruins

  • Several islands contain the ruins of Narfelli settlements from centuries past
  • Most have been deliberately abandoned—locals avoid them after dark
  • Some ruins show signs of infernal rituals or demonic habitation
  • Archaeological value is significant but danger is extreme

Activities & Resources

Fishing

Fishing is the primary reason outsiders visit Icelace Lake. The abundance of fish—particularly salmon and trout—has sustained the Nar peoples for generations.

Traditional Methods

  • Deerskin Canoes: Traditional Nar fishing vessels; lightweight and maneuverable
  • Fish Traps: Elaborate weirs built in shallow inlets to funnel fish
  • Net Fishing: Used in deeper waters during spawning runs
  • Ice Fishing: Holes cut in winter ice; dangerous but effective
  • Spear Fishing: Traditional method requiring skill and patience

Commercial Potential

  • Salted Fish: Major trade commodity; preserves well for long journeys
  • Fish Oil: Used for lamps, cooking, and preservation
  • Furs: Bears, wolves, and otters provide valuable pelts
  • Bone & Ivory: Fish bones worked into tools and ornaments

Fishing Hazards

  • Hypothermia: Can occur in minutes if canoe tips in winter
  • Predatory Fish: Giant pike can overturn boats or strike unwary fishermen
  • Weather Changes: Sudden storms can develop in minutes
  • Bears: Especially dangerous during salmon runs; may attack fishing camps
  • Thin Ice: Winter ice can be deceptively fragile in certain areas

Travel & Trade Routes

The lake serves as both a barrier and a highway, depending on the season and the traveler's capabilities.

Water Routes

  • Summer Travel: Boats can navigate the open water; journey across takes 5-7 days
  • Coastal Routes: Following the shore takes longer but is safer and allows supply stops
  • River Inlets: Several rivers feed the lake; can be followed inland for trade

Ice Routes (Winter Only)

  • Direct Crossing: Possible with proper equipment; 1-2 days across the widest point
  • Dangers: Thin spots, invisible cracks, whiteouts, cold exposure
  • Guidance: Local Nar provide winter guides for significant payment

Land Routes

  • Around the Lake: Longer but safer; follows established trails and settlements
  • Mountain Passes: Shorter but extremely dangerous; avalanches and uncharted territory

Dangers & Hazards

Environmental Threats

Cold & Hypothermia

  • Water Temperature: 32-45°F year-round
  • Time to Hypothermia: 15-30 minutes in water without protection
  • Constitution Saves: DC 12 every 5 minutes in cold water
  • Failure: Gain one level of exhaustion

Ice Hazards

  • Thin Spots: Areas of 6 inches or less can collapse under weight
  • Pressure Ridges: Jagged ice formations can overturn sleds and damage equipment
  • Melt Holes: Even in winter, weak ice creates sudden breaks
  • Whiteout Conditions: Blowing snow obscures landmarks and orientation

Weather

  • Sudden Storms: Can develop in 30 minutes; winds exceed 50 mph
  • Fog Banks: Roll in from the glacier; visibility drops to 10 feet
  • Wind Chill: Can drop effective temperature to -30°F or lower

Wildlife Threats

  • Bears (Spring/Summer): Aggressive during salmon runs; view humans as competitors
  • Giant Pike: Can bite through boat hulls; 8-12 feet long specimens reported
  • Wolves: Rare but deadly; hunt in packs along frozen shores in winter
  • Dire Bears: Occasional appearances in deepest winter; enormous and feral

Lore & Mysteries

Local Legends

The Lake's Curse

Local Nar speak of an old curse on the lake, supposedly connected to Narfelli infernalists who tried to bind a water elemental in the lake centuries ago. The ritual went wrong, they say, and something was trapped beneath the ice. Fishermen report:

  • Strange lights under the ice in winter
  • Waters that move against the current
  • Boats found empty, as if their crews simply vanished
  • Fish with unusual mutations or unnatural colors

The Deep One

Older Nar refuse to fish in the deepest central basin, speaking of something "old and large" that dwells there. They describe:

  • A shape glimpsed in the water's depths during summer
  • Ice that cracks from underneath in patterns that seem almost purposeful
  • The water sometimes taking on an oily, iridescent quality
  • Fishermen who ventured too deep and returned... changed somehow

Ghost Rock Island

This island's ruins are known to be cursed. Local tales say:

  • It was a Narfelli military outpost during their empire
  • The garrison was destroyed in a demonic incursion
  • Soldiers' spirits still guard the ruins
  • The ruins contain infernal artifacts of considerable power and danger
  • Survivors report hearing screams on the wind despite the island's emptiness

The Winter Wars

Occasionally, the deep ice breaks and reveals ancient bodies or weapons from conflicts fought centuries ago. Scholars believe:

  • Narfelli forces fought Raumathar armies on the frozen lake
  • Powerful magic was used, possibly causing permanent damage
  • Entire armies were lost, their remains still preserved in ice

Adventure Hooks

Quests Involving Icelace Lake

Low-Level (Exploration)

  • Fishing Expedition: Help Whitefin Tribe catch enough salmon for winter storage
  • Safe Passage: Guide merchants across the lake safely during spring thaw
  • Bear Hunt: Track and scare off a bear threatening a fishing camp (not combat)
  • Supply Run: Retrieve trade goods from a settlement on the opposite shore

Mid-Level (Investigation)

  • The Missing Crew: Investigate why fishing boats have been disappearing
  • Ghost Rock Expedition: Explore the ruins cautiously, gather information about the curse
  • Winter Crossing: Journey across the frozen lake; deal with hazards and encounters
  • The Deep Mystery: Strange occurrences in the lake's center—investigate
  • Ancient Weapons: Ice breaks reveal weapons from old war; recover artifacts for study

High-Level (Resolution)

  • Banish the Water Elemental: Properly bind or destroy the creature trapped in the lake
  • Break the Curse: Perform ritual to cleanse the lake of infernal contamination
  • The Deep One's True Nature: Confront whatever dwells in the depths
  • Clear Ghost Rock: Definitively end the curse on the island and retrieve artifacts
  • Restore the Nar Settlement: Stabilize and expand Whitefin Landing

Ongoing Complications

  • Seasonal Changes: Summer and winter present different challenges and opportunities
  • Wildlife Migrations: Seasonal salmon runs and bear activity affect travel and safety
  • Nar Politics: Tribal tensions over fishing rights and trade routes
  • External Threats: Raiders, mercenaries, or cultists from distant lands
  • Resource Competition: Multiple groups seeking the lake's fish, furs, and ancient treasures

DM Notes

Running Icelace Lake

Atmosphere

The lake should feel vast, isolating, and dangerous:

  • Emphasize the endless horizon and the sense of exposure on open water or ice
  • Describe the silence of the frozen lake—eerily quiet in winter
  • Use temperature as a constant threat and clock (how long can they last?)
  • Add sensory details: the crunch of ice, the calls of distant birds, the smell of fish
  • Make the Nar feel like locals with deep knowledge of a harsh but survivable land

Seasonal Variations

  • Spring (Thaw): Water access but dangerous ice breaks; salmon runs; bears aggressive
  • Summer: Open water navigation; comfortable temperature; maximum travel difficulty minimal
  • Autumn (Pre-Freeze): Final fishing season; weather becoming unstable; freezing begins
  • Winter (Frozen): Solid ice; extreme cold; isolation; limited daylight

Scaling Difficulty

  • Level 1-4: Focus on environmental challenges and Nar interactions; avoid major monsters
  • Level 5-8: Introduce wilderness encounters; minor supernatural elements; possible creature sightings
  • Level 9-12: Explore Ghost Rock ruins; confront lake mysteries; deal with larger threats
  • Level 13+: Encounter major supernatural threats; break ancient curses; explore deepest secrets

Environmental Rules

  • Cold Exposure: Constitution save DC 10 every hour; failure = one level exhaustion
  • Hypothermia: In water, save becomes DC 12 and happens every 5 minutes
  • Winter Crossing: Requires navigation checks and awareness of thin ice hazards
  • Bears: Present but avoidable; become aggressive if provoked or food is left accessible

Magical Considerations

  • Fire spells may be affected by the frigid environment (discuss with players)
  • Ice magic is more potent here; cold-based attacks gain advantage
  • Water breathing spells must contend with the numbing cold
  • Infernal magic shows signs of its presence—faint sulfurous smells, unnatural reactions

NPC Relationships

The Nar tribes have distinct personalities and motivations:

  • Whitefin Tribe: Hospitable traders; want to protect their fishing grounds
  • Glacierbane Tribe: Suspicious; test newcomers' competence and courage
  • Rivermount Tribe: Curious about outside world; good sources of information
