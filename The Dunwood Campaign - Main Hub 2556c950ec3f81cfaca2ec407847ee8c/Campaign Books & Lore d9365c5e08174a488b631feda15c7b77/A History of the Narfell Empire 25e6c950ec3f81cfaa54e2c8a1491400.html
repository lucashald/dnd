Chapter VII: The Demon Markets of Dun-Tharos

At the height of Narfell's dominance, the great citadel of Dun-Tharos had a bustling marketplace where mortal ambition traded freely with infernal power. What follows is an account of the daily commerce that defined the empire's relationship with the Lower Planes, based on surviving merchant records and the testimony of bound spirits.

The Morning Bell and the Summoning Courts

Each dawn in Dun-Tharos began with the ringing of the Brass Bell of Binding, forged from metal that had never seen sunlight and inscribed with the names of ninety-nine different demon princes. As its tone echoed through the citadel's black stone corridors, merchants, nobles, and common citizens alike would gather in the Summoning Courts—seven circular chambers built into the citadel's lower levels, each dedicated to trafficking with a different tier of infernal hierarchy.

The First Court handled what the Nentyarchs termed "common commerce"—minor imps trading information for copper coins blessed by dark priests, quasits offering their services as spies in exchange for written contracts sealed in blood, and lemures bartering their crude labor for the promise of eventual freedom. A typical morning might see a merchant securing the services of a dozen lemures to work his quarries, paying not in gold but in signed promissory notes that would claim the souls of his workers upon their natural deaths.

The Hierarchy of Infernal Exchange

The higher courts dealt in increasingly sophisticated transactions. In the Third Court, lesser devils would arrive bearing exotic goods from the Nine Hells—metals that burned without heat, gems that whispered secrets to their owners, and silks woven from shadow itself. Payment for such treasures required increasingly elaborate contracts, often spanning multiple generations and including clauses that modern legal scholars find both ingenious and horrifying.

Master Merchant Kaladar Thrice-Bound, whose memoir survived the citadel's fall, describes a typical transaction in the Fifth Court: "The cornugon arrived precisely at the stroke of noon, as stipulated in our previous agreement. It bore three vials of liquid starlight, extracted from dying celestials, in exchange for the promised consideration—the accumulated dreams of fifty children from the Moonshae Isles, collected over the course of seven winters. The contract required seventeen separate seals, three blood signatures, and the witnessed testimony of two other devils of equal rank. The entire process consumed four hours and required the services of six different interpreters of infernal law."

The Scribes of Binding

Perhaps no group was more essential to Narfell's infernal commerce than the Scribes of Binding—a guild of specialists who dedicated their lives to mastering the intricate legal frameworks that governed demon contracts. These individuals, marked by the characteristic pale complexion that came from spending decades in the citadel's windowless contract chambers, possessed an almost supernatural understanding of infernal linguistics and the subtle gradations of demonic hierarchy.

A junior scribe might spend his entire career learning to properly address a single rank of devil, while master scribes were said to speak seventeen different dialects of Infernal and could identify the specific subspecies of any demon simply by observing the grammatical structures they employed in their binding oaths. The guild maintained a library containing over three thousand volumes of precedent, cross-referenced by demon type, contract duration, and the specific nature of the consideration offered.

The Toll of Prosperity

Yet this prosperity came at a cost that extended far beyond the obvious price of souls and suffering. The constant presence of infernal entities within the citadel began to corrupt the very stone of Dun-Tharos itself. Walls wept blood during certain lunar phases, shadows moved independently of their casters, and the air in the lower levels grew so thick with malevolent energy that unprotected visitors would experience waking nightmares simply by breathing it.

The corruption spread outward from the citadel like ripples in a dark pond. Villages within fifty miles of Dun-Tharos reported increases in stillbirths, livestock born with extra limbs or human-like faces, and wells that ran red during the winter months. The forest that would later become known as the Dunwood began to twist and darken, its trees growing in unnatural spirals and its wildlife developing a taste for carrion and decay.

The Feast of Screaming Stars

The height of Narfell's infernal commerce occurred during the annual Feast of Screaming Stars, held during the winter solstice when the barriers between planes grew thin. For seven days and nights, the Summoning Courts would remain open continuously, hosting an otherworldly bazaar that drew demons, devils, and worse creatures from across the Lower Planes.

Contemporary accounts describe streets filled with beings that hurt to look upon, the air alive with the sound of languages that predated human civilization, and transactions that involved the buying and selling of abstract concepts—colors that had never been seen, sounds that could only be heard by the dying, and emotions so pure they could drive mortals to madness simply by proximity.

The Feast's final night traditionally culminated in the Great Binding—a massive ritual in which the Nentyarch himself would bind a powerful demon to serve the empire for the coming year. These entities, known as the Nentyarch's Hounds, served as generals in Narfell's armies and were responsible for many of the empire's most devastating military victories.
